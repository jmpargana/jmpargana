Have you ever felt like you take more than you give?

I've been using free information and resources for as long as I remember. My journey with computers started with linux. Most of the software I've used ever since (I'd guess about 99%) has been open source. I've read open documentation, downloaded free books, learned how to code with free resources. Everything for free. When it wasn't free I used torrents or libgen (I hope this admission is not enough to charge me). I've build my knowledge and tools from things that are out there. 

Well, in order for things to be out there, someone is publishing them. Someone is spending time and effort writing the documentation, developing, testing, fixing, publishing the code and tools we all use. The vast majority of those people do so without getting paid, outside of their work, on their free time. Time they could use consuming. Instead, they contribute. They contribute to us all. 

My interest in open source as a contributor is two fold. I've felt guilty that I only take on one hand. On another I know there's a certain merit to being an open source contributor. People take pride. They are respected and get reputation within the software engineering communities. 

I wanted to be part of that community but not really understanding what it means. Now, I see that contributors are skilled enough to get to know a system managed by people they don't know, who just follow conventions and principles that govern each individual project. Being a contributor means you can adapt to a new system. It also means you are comfortable and confident you can improve a project with changes that will have the highest quality, will follow the best standards and will be used by people who like me, will be thankful someone put their time and effort working on something.

## Getting started

It can be a daunting task. Especially your first contribution. If you look for big projects on Github, you'll find highly complex source code, a lot of documentation with guidelines, thousands of issues with tens of comments. If you investigate who the commenters and maintainers are, you'll sometimes spot people in big tech companies, principal engineers, community giants. In a nutshell, it's intimidating. 

Don't be frighten. For the very first step. Let's call it step zero.
